The Public Opinions

Given the question encompassing whether the computer revolution made its impact positively towards society, the most obvious answer would be "yes." Anybody would be right to answer in that sense; however, there are more deeper connections about this topic through what made society better or worse which people haven't had made much thought about.


Let's start off with the common knowledge people know about the positives of the computer revolution. We already know society became better because of the massive convenience and efficiency from computers, it saves time and energy to produce data within large amounts. For example, everyday people use computer software applications like spreadsheets or documents to organize information, it became streamlined into many jobs and businesses. Next, we know that society became better through various methods of communication, no matter how far the sender is from the receiver. The computer revolution shortly later developed emails, and then years later developed social media. Collaboration between people became possible through video conferences as the development of computers continued over the years. That's not all, the computer revolution made its contributions towards education and scientific research. In fact, there was a 1999 survey done at DePauw University by Dennis A. Trinkle that supports the fact that many college students and professors use computers to learn, research, and teach their subject content (in the case of the survey, it was specifically towards history). The list goes on, but the main point here is that the computer revolution had made an overarching domino effect on society over many decades, and the mass majority of the public sides in agreement that the benefits outweigh the costs.



Now, what do I mean by the costs? Despite the overwhemling positives the computer revolution as brought upon people, there are others who think that the revolution has negatively impacted society in the future. Not a mistake in any means, but a mistreatment of computer usage by despicable people called criminals. Computers in the future become suspectible to cybersecurity risks, meaning information could be easily obtained to those who are knowledgeable to hacking into computer systems. Aside from the criminal activities they bring to computers, there are hidden negative impacts that hurts towards society. Due to the automation of computers since the revolution, many industry jobs are overtaken by computers (not to be confused with the reigning terror of artifical intelligence we have today) for faster efficiency that a typical human cannot handle like information or data. Another thing that has negatively impacted society is how computers has possession of people's daily lives, causing mental addiction to the screen and creating poor physical health. The list also goes on, but the main point here is that the computer revolution's slow growth and attention affected the future of society negatively, and a small majority of the public has compelling arguments that people's unmoral actions through computers made it outweigh the benefits.


Laying out both sides of the argument, there are those who think that the computer revolution's impact had nothing for society due to the balancing positives and negatives the revolution brought. If the computer revolution did nothing to society, does that mean that the revolution hasn't happened yet? Alan C. Kay in 1997 made a talk about how the the computer revolution is still developing improvements and adding features for computers for the next oncoming future. He makes a few key points that computers are shrinking in size capacity, the architecture of the computer changes, the programming languages of the computer are advancing, and the computers are expanding their potential to the next level. While may these sound like they're beneficial for the long-run, the problem arises that programmers would have to adapt to these new challenges, the overall complexity of the computer system is getting harder to comprehend. Because of this, there is sort of "arms race" between the development of the computer and the programmer to create programs that leads the computer revolution to not happen yet. The fullest potential hasn't been met yet, and it continues to be true through the development of artifical intelligence that people are studying today. The computer revolution is making its impact on society gradually better while simultaneously making society worse, and that is where the balance between the revolution's positives and negatives comes from.
